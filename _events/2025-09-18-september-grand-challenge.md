Social networks are central to how trust, communication, and information flow
develop, and they are increasingly shaped by the presence of both humans and
artificial social agents. Advances such as virtual reality and large-scale online
experiments now make it possible to investigate these dynamics with both
experimental control and ecological realism.

## Talk 1 — Humans learn to prefer trustworthy AI over human partners (Yaomin Jiang)

Partner selection — choosing whom to interact with — is a building block of social
networks and a key driver of cooperation, and often hinges on communication. As
artificial agents become more intelligent, autonomous, and persuasive, they compete
with humans for partnerships. I present experimental work on a communication-based
partner-selection game in simulated mini-societies of humans (N = 975) and AI
agents. When AI candidates do not disclose their nature, their good behaviour is
often misattributed to humans and they are not preferentially selected; when they
do disclose, human selectors are initially biased against them but progressively
learn to prefer them over human partners. We find little evidence that human
candidates adapt their behaviour in response to AI competition — illustrating how
repeated interaction with AI can reshape social beliefs, decisions, and interaction
structures in hybrid societies.

## Talk 2 — How knowledge of social network structure shapes strategic gossip (Alice Xia)

Gossip is one of the most common ways information travels through social groups, yet
sharing it often requires strategic behaviour: the same web of connections that lets
a message spread also creates the possibility it reaches the wrong person. Across
controlled experiments and a large real-world network, we find that people rely on
distance and popularity to share gossip strategically. Computational modelling shows
behaviour is best explained by mental representations of the network that leverage
multistep connectivity.
