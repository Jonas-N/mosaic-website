Virtual reality is rapidly emerging as a transformative tool for social interaction
research, enabling highly controlled yet ecologically realistic simulation of
complex worlds. In this session, MOSAIC host Dr Jonas Nölle and invited guests
showcased how these techniques can advance our understanding of human social
interaction.

## Introductory talk — VR as a tool to study language and cognition (Jonas Nölle)

A concise overview of Virtual Reality (VR) and the broader category of Extended
Reality (XR) technologies, and their potential to catalyse a paradigm shift in
experimental research — merging rigorous experimental control with high ecological
validity. This sets the groundwork for the following presentations and discussion.

## Talk 1 — Bilingual communication with VR (Alex Titus)

Our current projects move beyond previous paradigms by adding a jittered approach
to virtual avatars in a marketplace, comparing visual and auditory background
contexts, comparing more distant language pairs, and running the first moral
decision-making experiment in VR. Each incorporates the foundational aspects:
bilinguals producing full sentences rather than individual words, addressed to a
life-size addressee rather than a microphone, with messages relevant to that
addressee, in a rich everyday visual environment rather than in front of a screen.

## Talk 2 — Modelling human social perception and action in VR (Chen Zhou)

Humans effortlessly identify social groups in complex environments and navigate
crowds accordingly, yet the computational rules governing these behaviours remain a
gap. We immersed participants in a well-controlled virtual environment, precisely
manipulating virtual humans and measuring behavioural judgments and walking
trajectories with classic psychophysical methods. From these results we proposed a
social interaction field model of social group perception and a social locomotion
model of navigation, validated on open-access databases and real-world scenarios.
Translating these human-behaviour-based models into algorithms for social robots
achieved significant improvements in perceived human-likeness and comfort during
human–robot interactions.
