This session discussed what the different modalities — manual gestures,
vocalisations, and facial expressions — fundamentally contribute to human
communication. It uniquely took place in the morning to accommodate Nic Fay's
Australian time zone.

## What's in a face? Semiotic capacities of facial expressions (Jonas Nölle)

A brief introduction to the session, showcasing recent research from the
FACESYNTAX ERC project. I argue that facial expressions possess greater semiotic
potential than is commonly acknowledged, and raise two questions for discussion:
Are facial expressions iconic? And do they form part of the same system as
gestures?

## Evidence that gesture is the primary modality for language creation (Nicolas Fay)

Laboratory simulations are increasingly used to inform theories of language
origin. Using a "charades"-style referential game, I find that communication is
twice as successful in the gesture modality than the (non-linguistic) vocal
modality. This holds for adults and children, blind participants, and even
cross-culturally. I argue that the success of gesture is due to its greater
affordance for iconic and embodied signs.

## Vocalizations afford plenty of iconicity (Marcus Perlman)

Do gestures afford more iconicity than vocalizations? I argue this is the wrong
question. Vocalizations afford plenty of iconicity — more than enough to get a
symbol system off the ground with a little time. The far more interesting
questions lie in how and why iconic communication is multimodal, spread across the
hands and body, face, and voice.
