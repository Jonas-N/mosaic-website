The first Grand Challenge of 2025 was a transatlantic session on **hyperscanning**
— a neuroimaging technique that simultaneously measures the brain activity of
multiple individuals during social interactions.

## Talk 1 — Neural Mechanisms Underlying Hyperscanning (Antonia Hamilton)

Interpersonal neural synchrony has been described in many contexts where brain
activity is recorded from two or more people simultaneously. However, there has
been controversy over how to interpret this signal. Here I present a mutual
prediction model of hyperscanning effects and argue that these studies can be
informative even if there is no direct cross-brain causal mechanism.

## Talk 2 — Hyperscanning: Theory, Methods, and Applications (Guillaume Dumas)

Hyperscanning provides a comprehensive framework for examining the neural
underpinnings of social interaction. This talk offers an overview of the
theoretical foundations, methodologies, and applications. Anchored in social
psychology and embodied cognition, the framework emphasises the interdependence of
social and neural processes. We explore various hyperscanning modalities — their
strengths, limitations, and inter-brain connectivity measures — and introduce
biophysical and deep-learning models to explain and quantify inter-brain
connectivity. Finally we discuss applications in mental health, toward an
"inter-personalized psychiatry", and present a social neuro-AI roadmap for how
multi-brain neuroscience may inspire a paradigm shift in machine learning.

## Speakers

**Prof. Antonia Hamilton** leads the Social Neuroscience group at the Institute of
Cognitive Neuroscience, UCL, and is Editor-in-Chief of the *Quarterly Journal of
Experimental Psychology*. Her research interests include how and why people imitate
each other, how social skills differ in autism, and the neural mechanisms of
face-to-face social interaction.

**Prof. Guillaume Dumas** is Associate Professor of Computational Psychiatry at the
Université de Montréal and PI of the Precision Psychiatry and Social Physiology
Laboratory at the CHU Sainte-Justine Research Centre. His research cross-fertilises
AI, cognitive neuroscience, and digital medicine, including "Social Neuroscience
for AI".
