From spider dances to human language, communication systems face the same
fundamental problem: why should anyone believe a signal when deception can pay?
Signalling theory tells us that honesty has to be enforced — by cost, by context,
or by something deeper in the architecture of communication itself. In this
session, we explore how trust became a design feature of human language, how
multimodality keeps signals honest across species, and what these evolutionary
perspectives reveal about deception, cooperation, and communication today.

## Invited hosts

**Sławomir Wacewicz** (Nicolaus Copernicus University, Toruń) is Associate
Professor of Linguistics and co-founder of the Center for Language Evolution
Studies (CLES), where he currently serves as deputy head. He also directs the
Toruń Interactionist Lab (TIL), which investigates the dynamics of human
interaction. His work on the cost–benefit calculus of communication has shaped
current thinking on the origins of language, including his notion of a “platform
of trust” as a foundational condition for language to evolve at all.

**Séverine Hex** (University of Exeter; Junior Fellow, Harvard Society of Fellows)
is a comparative ethologist studying how animals in complex societies use
multimodal communication and social bonds to navigate their social worlds. She
presents her new *Behavioral and Brain Sciences* target article, “Multimodality
as a safeguard of honesty in communication and language: from Animals to Humans”,
which argues that combining signals across modalities is one of evolution’s key
solutions to the honesty problem — from animal displays to human language.
