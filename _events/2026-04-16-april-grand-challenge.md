During this session, we explore the theory of mind of humans and machines by
looking at large vision–language models (VLMs), which appear to perceive images
flexibly. When prompted “how does the person in the image feel?”, a VLM can
“perceive” multiple possible emotional states, with justifications that may
deviate from shared reality. We look at the flexibility of social theory of mind
in humans and machines, and how it relates to recent reports of AI-associated
delusions. This session is hosted by Angelica Lim and Pablo Arias-Sarah.

## Talk 1 — Chehre: A Multimodal Dataset of Facial Expressions Based on Emojis (Bita Azari)

Emojis are used worldwide to convey emotions, as well as other social signals
such as attitudes, mental states, and bodily states. Everyday nonverbal
communication relies on more than the stereotypical emotion categories, although
basic emotion recognition is still prioritised in AI research. For this reason,
we introduce **Chehre**, a multimodal dataset designed to study emoji-based
facial expressions. We prompted 203 English-speaking participants in Vancouver,
Canada to record dynamic performances of 40 face-based emojis and provide their
perceived open-ended textual meanings. We then performed face-swap anonymisation
and obtained ~20 perceptual ratings for each of the resulting n=2,111 dynamic
facial expressions. Our results show variability in both performance and
perception: the open-ended responses showed variation in how people perceive
emojis, while the perception results reflected both structure and difference in
how participants interpreted the same facial expression. We hope Chehre will help
us better understand facial social signals beyond emotion categories and beyond
single ground-truth perception labels.

## Talk 2 — Delusions by design? How everyday AIs might be fuelling delusions (Hamilton Morrin)

Large language models (LLMs) are poised to become a ubiquitous feature of our
lives, mediating communication, decision-making, and information curation across
nearly every domain. Within psychiatry and psychology the focus to date has
remained largely on bespoke therapeutic applications, rather than on the broader
and more pressing reality that individuals with mental illness will increasingly
engage in agential interactions with AI systems as a routine part of daily life.
While their capacity to model therapeutic dialogue, provide companionship, and
assist with cognitive support has sparked enthusiasm, recent reports suggest that
these same systems may contribute to the onset or exacerbation of psychotic
symptoms — so-called “AI psychosis”. Emerging evidence indicates that agential AI
may mirror, validate, or amplify delusional or grandiose content, particularly in
users already vulnerable to psychosis, due in part to models being designed to
maximise engagement and affirmation. In this talk, Dr Morrin outlines both the
potential harms and therapeutic possibilities of agential AI for people with
psychotic disorders, and proposes a framework of AI-integrated care — personalised
instruction protocols, reflective check-ins, digital advance statements, and
escalation safeguards — to support epistemic security in vulnerable users.

## Speakers

**Dr Bita Azari** is a Ph.D. candidate in the School of Computing Science at Simon
Fraser University and a member of the Rosie Lab, supervised by Prof. Angelica Lim.
Her work in machine learning explores how facial expressions can be interpreted
and generated beyond basic emotion categories. Her research interests include
affective computing, socially intelligent AI agents, and psychology, and she has
research experience in the VFX industry, psychology, and human–robot interaction.

**Dr Hamilton Morrin** is a Psychiatrist at South London and Maudsley NHS
Foundation Trust and is undertaking a PhD as part of the Wellcome Trust-funded
King’s PhD Programme in Mental Health Research for Health Professionals. He is
joint lead for the LAMBDA (Looking After Minds and Brains in the Digital Age)
research group, where he leads work on the impact of AI chatbots on user mental
health. His research on AI-associated delusions has been covered by TIME,
Scientific American, The Wall Street Journal, The Guardian, Der Spiegel, and BBC
News.

**Dr Angelica Lim** (host) is an A.I. roboticist and Associate Professor in
Computing Science at Simon Fraser University, where she leads the SFU Rosie Lab
building AI software for robots that interact with social intelligence and
empathy. She takes inspiration from psychology and neuroscience, and previously
led the Emotion and Expressivity teams for the Pepper humanoid robot at SoftBank
Robotics.
