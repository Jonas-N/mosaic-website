Our invited guest was **Prof. Aslı Özyürek**, Director of the recently founded
Multimodal Language Department at the Max Planck Institute for Psycholinguistics
in Nijmegen. Her research uses cross-linguistic, developmental, and experimental
approaches to study speech, gesture, and sign, and challenges the traditional view
of language as a unimodal communication system.

## Abstract

Most theories about the nature of human language — how it is processed, acquired,
and evolved — have assumed that language can be characterised by what is
expressible in speech or text: a single channel, arbitrary form–meaning mappings,
and sequential organisation. However, all the world's languages embody visible
components: spoken languages are accompanied by manual and facial gestures, and
language can be expressed by signed as well as spoken languages.

In this talk I explain how taking into account these universally shared visible
components, and their modality-specific characteristics such as iconicity, visible
indexicality, and simultaneity (e.g. saying "play" in speech while performing a
piano-playing gesture at the same time), changes the way we view and revisit our
assumptions about language structure, use in interaction, (neural) processing,
acquisition, and evolution. Presenting evidence from research in different spoken
and signed languages, I argue that a multimodal view of language can explain its
adaptive structure, resilience, and learnability across contexts, cultures, and
individual variation.
