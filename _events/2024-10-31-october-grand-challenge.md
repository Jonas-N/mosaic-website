The **Visual Language Lab** (Tilburg University) discussed the multimodal nature of
language by drawing insights from the visual languages used in comics and their
relationships to the structures of spoken languages.

## Abstract

Multimodality necessitates both the incorporation of all natural modalities into a
common architecture and a rethinking of modalities' fundamental relations to each
other. Here we explore typological relations between the vocal and graphic
modalities using the TINTIN Corpus, an annotated corpus of 1,030 comics from 144
countries. First, Neil Cohn outlines a multimodal cognitive architecture and its
reconceptualization of diversity, universals, and relativity, and demonstrates the
tensions between these issues in the framing structure of 76,000 panels from the
corpus. Next, in "Whoosh! Visual depictions of motion events", Irmak Hacımusaoğlu
explores the lexicalized patterns of depicted motion events in 315 comics, and how
basic linguistic constructs of motion events are typologically represented in
graphic depictions. Finally, in "Iconicity and frequency of lines depicting
motion in comics", Ana Krajinović examines motion-event cues by comparing four
types of symbolic motion lines by average size and frequency, and their
diagrammatic and residual imagic iconicity. Altogether, we highlight how similar
typological issues persist in the "visual languages" of comics and those of other
linguistic systems.
